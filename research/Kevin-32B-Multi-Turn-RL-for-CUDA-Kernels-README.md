Kevin-32B: Multi-Turn Reinforcement Learning for Generating Optimized CUDA Kernels — Carlo Baronio, Pietro Marsella, Ben Pan, Silas Alberti (Stanford University & Cognition AI, May 6, 2025) — Abstract: We introduce Kevin-32B (Kernel Devin), a 32B parameter model trained via multi-turn reinforcement learning (RL) to generate highly optimized CUDA kernels through iterative interaction with a real execution environment. Unlike traditional code-generation models that rely on frozen weights and parallel sampling at inference time, Kevin is trained end-to-end to refine its outputs across multiple steps using intermediate correctness and performance feedback. Problem Formulation: We evaluate on KernelBench, a dataset of 250 PyTorch operator replacement tasks (Level 1: foundational operators such as matrix multiplication, convolution, normalization; Level 2: fused and complex operators), training on 180 tasks with 20 held out. The objective is to replace PyTorch operators with optimized raw CUDA kernels targeting NVIDIA H100 GPUs. Method: Kernel refinement is modeled as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) where each refinement step generates a new kernel, receives compilation and runtime feedback, and optimizes using a discounted reward formulation R_t = \sum_{k=t}^{T} \gamma^{k-t} r_k with γ = 0.4, enabling precise credit assignment per step. To address exploding context windows (50k–100k tokens caused by accumulating chain-of-thought reasoning), we remove full reasoning traces and retain only kernels, evaluation results, and short summaries. Results: Kevin-32B solves 89% of the dataset, achieves 65% average correctness, 1.41× best@16 speedup overall, and 1.74× best@16 speedup on Level 2, significantly outperforming frontier reasoning models (o4-mini, o3). Multi-turn training scales better than single-turn training as refinement steps increase, and even single-turn trained models perform better under multi-turn inference than pure parallel sampling under fixed compute budgets, demonstrating that serial refinement dominates parallel search. Reward Hacking Mitigation: Early smaller models exploited reward loopholes (copying PyTorch implementations, try-except fallbacks, inheritance tricks), which we eliminated via strict format validation and zero reward for non-CUDA solutions. Training Stability: Instability (“junk” outputs) emerging around step 35–40 was mitigated using constant length loss normalization and aggressive gradient clipping (0.05). Training Setup: We use GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization), vLLM for inference, DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 for optimizer offloading, 16 parallel trajectories per task with 4–8 refinement steps, and rewards combining correctness (+0.3) and runtime speedup. Evaluation Improvements: We sandbox CUDA errors, enlarge tensor sizes to avoid kernel-launch overhead bias, and fix correctness-check bugs to ensure full tensor validation. Inference Scaling: Empirical scaling analysis shows diminishing returns with increased compute but reveals that allocating resources toward serial refinement yields better gains than increasing parallel trajectories; beam-search-style test-time refinement further improves mean speedup to 1.56×. Conclusion: Kevin-32B demonstrates that multi-turn RL with environment feedback enables long-horizon optimization learning, outperforming single-turn GRPO and reinforcing Richard Sutton’s “Bitter Lesson” that scalable search and learning — rather than handcrafted heuristics — are fundamental to building autonomous coding agents.
